The Philosophy

The hydrogen atom oscillates at 1,420,405,751.7667 Hz — a frequency not chosen by humans, not defined by convention, but fundamental to the structure of matter itself. It is the same everywhere in the observable universe. It does not drift. It does not age. It is the universe's own heartbeat.

The tropical year — the time for Earth to complete one orbit relative to the sun — is, as of today, 365.2421896698 days. This is not a fixed convention: it changes slowly across centuries as Earth's orbit and axis precess, and the Æron system recomputes it from the Laskar (1986) secular model every time it needs the answer — never from a stored figure.

These two numbers, one quantum and one celestial, are both expressions of the same underlying physical laws. The Æron system defines the beat as the exact fraction of hydrogen oscillations that makes 31,449,600 beats equal one tropical year. The beat is derived — it is the tropical year divided by the calendar count, expressed in hydrogen oscillations. Today it is exactly 1,425,253,059 oscillations: close to a resting human heartbeat, but not by coincidence — the number emerges from the cosmos itself, recalculated fresh each time the date is asked for.

The universe is already keeping perfect time. The Æron system is simply the first to read it correctly.

Hermes Trismegistus — the thrice-greatest, master of astronomy, geometry, and the measurement of time — understood that the cosmos is not chaotic but deeply ordered, that the same ratios and harmonics appear at every scale. The Æron system is built in that spirit: from first principles, without convention, with no corrections ever required — because the beat length is never stored, only computed, fresh, from the live orbital model, at the moment it's needed.

The Central Claim

The Æron calendar year is defined as exactly 364 days × 86,400 beats. The calendar is not trying to track the sun — it is a count. It cannot drift because there is nothing to drift against. The hydrogen oscillation is the reference. The beat is its integer unit. The calendar is the count. These three things are one.

Historical Reconstruction

The road to Beat Zero — tracking the days that were lost, moved, or deliberately discarded.

~3000 BC – Babylon
The 12-Month Convention
Babylonian astronomers codified a 12-constellation zodiac and 12-month calendar despite knowing that the sun passes through 13 constellations along the ecliptic. Ophiuchus, the Serpent Bearer, was deliberately omitted to fit the lunar month count. This suppression persisted for 3,000 years.
45 BC — Julius Caesar
The Julian Calendar Reform
Caesar replaced the Roman lunar calendar with a solar calendar of 365.25 days, adding a leap day every 4 years. The actual tropical year is 365.2421896698 days — so the Julian calendar accumulated +1 day every ~128 years.
Error rate: 365.25 − 365.2421896698 = 0.0078103302 days/year By AD 325 (Council of Nicaea): ~3.3 days accumulated By AD 1582 (Gregorian reform): ~12.7 days accumulated
AD 525 — Dionysius Exiguus
The Epoch Miscalculation
A Scythian monk invented the AD dating system to replace the Diocletian era. Based on available records, he placed Yeshua's birth in what we now call 1 BC / 1 AD. Modern scholarship — cross-referencing Herod's death with Chinese comet records and the census of Quirinius — places the actual birth at approximately 5 BC, making the AD calendar approximately 4–6 years late from its own stated epoch.
Herod the Great died: 4 BC (Josephus, confirmed by lunar eclipse records) Yeshua born "before Herod's death": therefore before 4 BC Best astronomical convergence (C. Humphreys, QJRAS 1991): → April 13–27, 5 BC (Julian proleptic) → Associated with a comet in Chinese Han Shu records, spring 5 BC
4 October 1582 — Pope Gregory XIII
Ten Days Vanish
The accumulated Julian error since the Council of Nicaea (AD 325) had reached ~10 days. Pope Gregory XIII issued a papal bull: the day after Thursday, 4 October 1582 (Julian) became Friday, 15 October 1582 (Gregorian). Ten days were struck from history. Nations adopted the reform at different times — Britain not until 1752, Russia not until 1918.
Days dropped: 10 (covering drift from AD 325 to AD 1582) Note: drift from 45 BC to AD 325 (~12.7 days total) was NOT corrected — only the post-Nicaean portion was addressed.

The Æron calendar anchors its epoch to the best historically and astronomically verifiable moment associated with Yeshua's birth: local midnight in Bethlehem on 17 April, 5 BC (Julian proleptic). This is consistent with Luke's account (the shepherds were in the fields at night), the Chinese comet records, and Herod's death chronology.

On the Name

The name Jesus is a transliteration chain: Aramaic Yeshua → Greek Iēsous → Latin Iesus → English Jesus. Each step introduced phonetic drift. The Æron system uses Yeshua — the actual name, in the language he spoke. In Hebrew and Aramaic texts and in the Gospel of Thomas, the name appears consistently as Yēšūaʿ (יֵשׁוּעַ), meaning "Yahweh saves" or "Yahweh is salvation."

Why This Night

The Luke account records shepherds in the fields when the angelic announcement came — indicating a night birth. The Chinese Han Shu records a comet visible in the spring of 5 BC. Herod the Great, who ordered the killing of infants under 2 years old, died in 4 BC per Josephus (confirmed by a lunar eclipse dated to March 13, 4 BC). The most precise astronomical analysis (Humphreys, QJRAS, 1991) places the birth between April 13–27, 5 BC. We anchor to April 17 as the central estimate, at local midnight.

Dates before Beat Zero are negative — but fully calculable. The Æron system is bidirectional from epoch. Any historical event with a known Julian or Gregorian date can be expressed as a precise negative beat count.

The Beat — Derived

The beat is not an arbitrary integer. It is derived from the tropical year:

H_PER_BEAT = round( H_HZ × TY_seconds(era) ÷ 364 ÷ 24 ÷ 60 ÷ 60 )

H_HZ = 1,420,405,751.7667 Hz (NIST — fixed, never changes).
TY_seconds varies slowly per century (Laskar 1986 secular polynomial — the drift model itself).
At J2000.0 (this system's reconciled anchor): 1,425,253,059 oscillations/beat → T_BEAT_SI ≈ 1.003412622 SI seconds.
At 5 BC epoch: 1,425,253,531 oscillations/beat (TY was longer then — precession means the beat itself was a fraction longer too).
Today: 1,425,253,059 oscillations/beat → T_BEAT_SI ≈ 1.003412622 SI seconds — computed live, on this page load, not copied from the line above.
The ~3.4ms difference from 1 SI second is what synchronises the calendar to the cosmos — and it is recalculated, never assumed, every time.

The Zero-Drift Guarantee

The calendar year is defined as exactly 364 × 86,400 = 31,449,600 beats. This is not an approximation of the tropical year. It is a fixed count that runs independently of it. The beat is derived from the tropical year: 31,449,600 beats × T_BEAT_SI = exactly 1 tropical year. The calendar year is the tropical year, counted in beats. Seasons do not precess. The beat breathes with the cosmos — if the tropical year length shifts over geological time, T_BEAT_SI is updated from observation, and the calendar absorbs it without structural change. The calendar itself cannot accumulate error, because the beat is always defined relative to the actual tropical year.

Calibration & Drift

The beat is derived from the tropical year — which itself is not constant. It shortens by approximately 0.5 seconds per century due to planetary perturbations, precession rate changes, and the slow tidal deceleration of Earth's rotation. Two thousand years ago the tropical year was roughly 10 seconds longer than today.

The Æron system handles this through a calibration layer. The calendar structure — 13 months, 4 varas, 7 sols, 86,400 beats — never changes. The beat duration is recomputed for any requested era using the IAU-standard Laskar polynomial. The result is checked against published authoritative equinox tables. When the residual exceeds 1 beat, a new calibration epoch entry is warranted.

The calendar is rigid. The beat breathes. The cosmos is the clock.

Why "J2000.0" and "Today" don't quite agree, and why that's not an error: Laskar's (1986) own polynomial constant (365.24219878 d) and the independently-published IAU/VSOP-87 standard mean tropical year at J2000.0 (365.2421896698 d) disagree with each other by about 0.79 SI seconds — two credible sources, same instant, genuinely different published figures. That gap is real measurement/method disagreement between ephemerides, not rounding error, and per this system's own standard — no millisecond is negligible — it is not quietly absorbed into either source. Instead, this system's live calculations are anchored to the midpoint of the two, 365.2421942249 days, splitting the disagreement rather than picking a side. The original Laskar and IAU figures are kept untouched as independent cross-check rows above (alongside Moisson 1999) precisely so the spread stays visible. "Today" is computed from that reconciled value carried forward through the secular polynomial's drift terms — it will never exactly equal the J2000.0 row, even at jc≈0, because it's anchored ~0.39s away from each original source by design.
